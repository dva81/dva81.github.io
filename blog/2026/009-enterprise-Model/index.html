The Enterprise Operating Model: Operating the Age of Intelligence

A New Perspective

For the last forty years, enterprises have learned how to manage people, processes and technology. Every technological wave, ERP, cloud, automation and now AI, has ultimately forced organizations to answer the same questions:

  • Who owns it?
  • Who runs it?
  • Who is accountable for it?
  • How do we measure it?
  • How resilient is it?
  • What business value does it create?

The questions have never changed. Only the capabilities have.

Perhaps that is why we should stop asking how to govern AI and start asking how enterprises should operate when intelligence becomes a fundamental organizational capability.

Key Principles

Leadership remains accountable for business outcomes.

Capabilities are the primary unit of ownership, not technologies.

Capabilities are composed of people, intelligence, automation and processes.

Operations includes governance, performance management and resilience by design.

AI is an integral component of enterprise capabilities rather than a separate workforce.

Organizations should design for continuity when intelligence does not perform as expected.

The Future Enterprise Operating Model

Capabilities Become the Unit of Ownership

Today’s organizations are structured around departments and teams.

Tomorrow’s organizations will continue to do so. What changes is not the organizational chart itself, but what constitutes a business capability.

Take Finance as an example.

The Finance Director is not responsible for:

  • Five financial controllers
  • Four AI agents
  • Twelve automations
  • Fourteen integrations

The Finance Director owns one thing:

The Finance Capability

That capability simply happens to consist of:

  • People
  • Intelligence
  • Processes
  • Technology

Leadership is still accountable for business outcomes regardless of how those outcomes are delivered.

Operations Becomes Capability Management

Operations should not be viewed as managing processes alone.

Operations manages capabilities.

This means understanding:

  • How are our teams performing?
  • How are our AI capabilities performing?
  • How resilient are our critical processes?
  • Are our platforms running as expected?
  • Where are our operational dependencies?
  • Are we creating business value?

In the age of intelligence, operations become capability management.

Governance is Not a Layer

One important realization is that governance, performance and resilience should not be represented as layers in the model.

They are enterprise-wide principles.

Governance applies equally to:

  • People
  • Intelligence
  • Processes
  • Technology

The same is true for:

  • Performance management
  • Resilience
  • Security
  • Compliance
  • Business continuity

These principles do not sit below operations. They surround and influence everything the enterprise does.

Closing Thought

Perhaps the most important realization is that this is not an AI framework.

It is an enterprise operating model.

For decades we have learned how to build software. Over the next twenty years, we will learn how to manage intelligence as an operational capability.

The organizations that succeed will not necessarily have the most AI. They will be the ones that understand how to run capabilities composed of people, intelligence, processes and technology to consistently deliver business value.

That is what running in the age of intelligence means.
